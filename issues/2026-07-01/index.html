This week's landscape — L&D + AI in 2026

The Big Picture This Week 🔭

Something is shifting in L&D — and it's not subtle. For years, the conversation was about using AI inside learning programs: generate a quiz, personalize a course path, automate reminders. That era is ending. Josh Bersin's February 2026 research put it plainly: AI doesn't just improve corporate learning, it replaces it. The $400 billion corporate training industry is facing the same disruption that hit publishing and travel booking — and it's happening faster than most L&D teams realize.

The companies winning right now aren't building better training programs. They're building what Bersin calls "dynamic enablement" — embedding skill development directly into the flow of work, driven by AI coaches and real-time skills intelligence. Meanwhile, internal mobility is getting a serious upgrade: 30–40% of your employees' skills are currently unknown or underutilized (HRTechCube, 2026). Mastercard figured this out and saved $21M. Most companies haven't.

And yet — fewer than 5% of organizations have actually deployed AI-native learning technology, per Bersin's data. There is a massive gap between where the narrative is and where the practice is. That gap is the opportunity. The companies that move in the next 12–18 months will build a structural advantage in how fast they can grow and retain talent.

Josh Bersin Feb 11, 2026

New Research: How AI Transforms $400 Billion of Corporate Learning

Bersin's fifth major L&D study dropped in February and it's the defining research of the year. The core finding: companies at Level 4 (AI-native, "Dynamic Enablement") are 10x more likely to be innovation leaders, 6x more likely to exceed financial targets, and 16x more likely to adapt well to change. The old model — build courses, publish to LMS, track completions — is dead. The new model is a living intelligence system where all company knowledge is searchable, queryable, and generative.

Bersin also breaks down the four maturity levels in detail: Static Training (Level 1, ~30% of market), Scaled Learning (Level 2, ~46%), Integrated Development (Level 3, shrinking), and Dynamic Enablement (Level 4, <5% today). The early movers are already seeing 40–50% reductions in L&D internal spend.

Key stat: 74% of companies tell us they are not keeping up with their company's demand for new skills — despite spending $400 billion on training. — Bersin Research, Feb 2026

Forbes Apr 13, 2026

14 AI-Powered Onboarding Strategies Agencies Are Using Right Now

One case study stands out: an agency built an internal AI assistant trained on their SOPs, client frameworks, and institutional knowledge. New hires ask it questions instead of interrupting senior staff. The result: onboarding time dropped from three months to roughly six weeks. But the bigger win, per the founder: new hires reported feeling more confident and less anxious — the AI was available at 2am when first-week nerves hit.

Other strategies include AI-generated personalized 30-60-90 day plans, proactive manager nudge systems, and predictive early-warning alerts that flag new hires showing disengagement signals before the 90-day mark. The pattern across all 14: AI doesn't replace the human connection in onboarding, it handles the repetitive information delivery so humans can focus on relationship-building.

Takeaway: Building an internal knowledge base that feeds an AI assistant is a low-cost, high-impact onboarding intervention replicable at most company sizes. No enterprise budget required.

TheHireHub.AI Apr 6, 2026

Internal Mobility 2026: AI Unlocks Your Hidden Talent

The data in this piece is striking. 30–40% of your employees' skills are either unknown to your organization or completely underutilized (HRTechCube, 2026). You are, in all likelihood, paying to recruit skills you already own. Mastercard built one of the most-cited examples: a talent marketplace that placed 75% of its workforce on an internal mobility platform, unlocking 100,000 hours of productive capacity and $21 million in documented cost savings.

The article also surfaces a critical cultural blocker: 60% of high-potential employees cite their immediate supervisor as the primary obstacle to internal movement (HRTechCube, 2026). Manager hoarding — protecting top performers to maintain team metrics — is rampant. AI matching helps, but the real intervention is rewiring manager incentives. Also: 51% of employees are unaware of internal opportunities at their organization (Gartner, 2026).

Key stat: 42.3% of organizations now use AI in their talent strategies — more than double the 17.9% in 2025. The adoption curve is steep. — SHRM State of AI in HR 2026

Josh Bersin Mar 2026

The World of Corporate Training Lurches Toward Enablement

This March piece crystallizes the strategic shift: training is an event, enablement is a system. Leading companies are dismantling their LMS-centric model and rebuilding around AI coaches, skills intelligence layers, and learning embedded in tools like Slack and Notion where work actually happens. The L&D team's job shifts from "content creator" to "performance architect." LinkedIn states that 70% of all job-related skills go out of date each year — which means a static course catalog is obsolete before it's published.

Bersin argues this is the most significant structural change to corporate learning in 20 years, and most L&D leaders are still measuring success in course completions. The early movers — Cisco, major insurance companies, large airlines — are building "Enablement Academies" per business unit with corporate HR owning only leadership, compliance, and culture programs.

Key stat: Early clients of AI-native learning platforms are seeing 40–50% reduction in L&D internal spend. — Bersin, Mar 2026

Market Logic Updated Jun 2026

Creating a Knowledge-Sharing Culture with AI — Dyson, REWE, Proximus

Three real case studies in this piece worth knowing. Dyson implemented an AI insights platform that gave every team "the big picture at the right time" — the platform acts as a single source of truth across all market research, eliminating information silos. REWE Group used AI-driven semantic search to surface insights across scattered data sets, dramatically reducing search time and enabling faster decisions. Proximus (Belgium's largest ICT provider) added multilingual AI search to break down departmental and language barriers.

The piece's most important insight: technology is not the hard part. The cultural barriers — fear of knowledge hoarding becoming visible, habits of information silos, manager resistance — are the real obstacle. Companies that succeed pair AI deployment with explicit change management and recognition for knowledge-sharing behavior.

Key stat: A solid knowledge-sharing platform can reduce time lost searching for information by up to 35% and boost productivity by 20–25%. — Market Logic, 2026

🍋 The Lemonade Angle

What this week's research means for L&D at Lemonade — specifically for product & engineering

1. Build the AI onboarding assistant now — before the next hiring wave

The Forbes case study (3 months → 6 weeks) isn't a moonshot. It's a structured knowledge base + an AI layer (Notion + a Slack bot, or a tool like Guru/Guru AI). Lemonade's engineering onboarding challenge — ramping engineers on a complex codebase, insurance domain logic, and internal tooling — is exactly the problem this solves. Start by auditing what your top 20 "first 30-day questions" are (ask recent joiners), document the answers, and build the assistant around those. The cultural win is real too: engineers stop feeling stupid for not knowing things, and seniors stop losing focus to repetitive interruptions.

2. The internal mobility play: position it as an engineering retention initiative, not HR process

The Mastercard data ($21M saved, 100K hours unlocked) and SHRM's numbers (42.3% of companies using AI in talent strategies now) give you a credible business case. At Lemonade's scale, you don't need Gloat to start. You need: (a) a quarterly "internal opportunity" signal — 1:1 conversation framework that asks every engineer about interest in adjacent areas (backend to ML, eng to PM, feature to platform), and (b) a lightweight skills inventory that's visible to recruiting and L&D. The cultural change is to make it safe for managers to develop talent for other teams. That requires explicit recognition, not just permission.

3. Skip the LMS — build a Bersin Level 4 from day one

Lemonade has the advantage of not having an entrenched LMS to migrate away from. The Bersin research shows early adopters of AI-native learning see 40–50% reduction in L&D internal spend vs traditional platforms. For a tech-focused team at Lemonade, this means: build learning into Slack and Notion (where work happens), use an AI tool like Sana or a custom Galileo-style layer to make institutional knowledge searchable and queryable, and measure success not in course completions but in time-to-productivity, skill application, and retention. The L&D team's job here is performance architecture, not content production.
