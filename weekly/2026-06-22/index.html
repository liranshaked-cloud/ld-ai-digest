Duolingo · GitHub customer story

Copilot + Codespaces, not a training rollout

Duolingo scaled from 3 core repos to 400+ as it moved to microservices, which fragmented code-review conventions across teams. Rather than a training program, they rolled out GitHub Copilot org-wide, built custom bots on GitHub's API to standardize workflows, and used Codespaces to kill local environment setup entirely. Named: Jonathan Burket (Sr. Eng Manager), Art Chaidarun (Principal Engineer), Severin Hacker (CTO).

📈 25% faster for engineers new to a repo · review time 3h→1h · setup: hours→~1 min

Vendor case study (GitHub promoting its own product) — but names real people and gives specific, checkable figures. Source →

💡 L&D Lesson: the highest-leverage "training" investment here wasn't a course — it was removing friction from the moment of actually doing the work. That's an L&D decision even though no one on the project had an L&D title.
Booking.com · via DX

When AI tool adoption stalls, the fix was enablement, not the tool

Booking.com's first AI coding assistant rollout underperformed — engineers were hesitant, usage lagged. The DevEx team (Bruno Passos, Zane Wright, Amos Haviv) got usage visibility via the DX platform, then built two-day hands-on workshops + recurring office hours, and reframed the goal from "adoption" to "frequent, effective use" (12+ days/month).

📈 +65% adoption after the push · daily users had 16% higher PR merge rate

Note: some secondary write-ups cite "3,500 engineers / 150,000 hours saved" for this rollout — we could not confirm those two numbers on DX's own case study pages, so we're leaving them out. Source →

💡 L&D Lesson: giving people the tool is not enablement. Booking.com only saw results after building the exact thing L&D exists to build — structured practice + ongoing support — instead of assuming access equals adoption.
Stripe · Atlas guide

"Stripe 101" + "/dev/start": real backlog tasks, not toy projects

Stripe replaced sink-or-swim onboarding with a company-wide program (82 internal instructors, founder Q&As) plus an engineering-specific layer: new engineers join temporary teams for 3-4 weeks, each paired with a senior mentor, working on real tasks pulled from actual team backlogs.

📈 94.5% rated first week positive · 90% understood the full stack by program end

Source →

💡 L&D Lesson: real backlog tasks beat toy projects because the mentor relationship and the actual work happen at the same time — this is the "3+ month ramp" problem this issue keeps coming back to, solved with a program design choice, not more content.

Adoption Without Judgment

The headline: L&D crossed a real threshold this year. Synthesia's 2026 survey puts AI adoption among learning teams at 87%, with only 2% saying they have no plans to adopt it at all. A March 2026 Chief Learning Officer analysis cites McKinsey data that 88% of organizations now use AI in at least one business function.

The catch: that same Chief Learning Officer piece cites Gallup's 22,000-person workforce survey — only 12% of employees actually use AI daily, despite that near-universal org-level adoption. 360Learning, citing Fosway Group research, found only 24% of promised vendor AI features are actually live. A Wharton study covered by Forbes: employees accepted incorrect AI answers 80% of the time, with high confidence in the wrong answer. And per LinkedIn's Workplace Learning Report, the share of employees whose manager helped them build a career plan just dropped to 15% — support is shrinking exactly when people need more of it, not less.

The fix: it isn't slowing adoption down — it's building the evaluation habit into the workflow itself, the same way IBM and Eightfold AI are mining real work samples for skills data instead of trusting self-reported profiles (see Case Files).

What this week means for Lemonade's product & engineering L&D

🔀 1. Build the "why trust this" habit into code review

The signalLemonade's engineers are already past the adoption question on AI coding tools. The real risk is an AI suggestion that looks confident and syntactically clean getting merged without a second look — especially risky on claims and underwriting logic.

The moveAdd a one-line PR template field for AI-assisted changes: "why do you trust this suggestion?" Cheap, fits the existing flow of work.

🗺️ 2. Treat the onboarding wiki as infrastructure, not content

The signalA 3+ month engineering ramp is a static-content problem, and Bersin's maturity model puts static content on the bottom rung.

The moveBuild a retrieval layer over what already exists — Slack history, incident runbooks, PR discussions — that answers a new engineer's actual question in the moment.

🧭 3. Mine GitHub activity for mobility signals, not a survey

The signalDuolingo and Stripe (see Case Files) show the highest-leverage moves happen inside the actual workflow, not a separate program.

The moveFor ~200 people in product and engineering, surface "who's actually been working near X" from real GitHub signals — languages touched, review patterns, on-call history — when a squad needs to staff a project or someone wants a lateral move.
