📝 Adoption Without Judgment

L&D crossed a real threshold this year. Synthesia's 2026 survey puts AI adoption among learning teams at 87%, with only 2% saying they have no plans to adopt it at all — this isn't an early-adopter story anymore, it's close to universal. TalentLMS's parallel 2026 benchmark finds 88% of HR managers now expect generative AI to fundamentally reshape how employees access knowledge at work.

Look one layer beneath the adoption number and a different story shows up. That same TalentLMS report finds 36% of employees say AI is actively weakening their independent problem-solving, and just 11% of learning leaders (per Absorb LMS's "Together" trends report) feel extremely confident in their organization's future skills-building strategy. A Wharton study covered by Forbes this week is more pointed: in controlled tests, employees accepted incorrect AI-generated answers 80% of the time — and did so with high confidence in the wrong answer. Josh Bersin frames the structural version of the same problem: 74% of companies say they aren't keeping pace with skill demand, despite roughly $400B a year going into corporate training.

Put together, this reads less like "AI transformation" and more like adoption that outran judgment. Organizations handed employees a powerful tool faster than they built the muscle to evaluate its output. Training Industry's new framework this week draws a useful line for L&D teams trying to fix this: AI literacy (safe, baseline, responsible use) is not the same as AI fluency (using AI for real advantage) — and most 2026 training programs are still teaching the former while employees need the latter.

The fix isn't slowing adoption down. It's building the evaluation habit into the workflow itself — the same way IBM and Eightfold AI are now mining real work samples for skills data instead of trusting self-reported profiles (more on that below). Trust the process less, and the output more.

🔍 Reality Check
What this is: every issue, we trace one widely-shared stat back to its original source and show what we find — the point is to model the exact "check before you trust it" habit this issue is about.

A widely-shared 2026 stat claims Mastercard's internal AI mobility platform delivered "$21M in savings, 100,000 hours unlocked, 75% of the workforce enrolled." We traced it back to the primary source — a 2022 Josh Bersin Company / Gloat case study — and the real, verified numbers are more modest: 62% of employees registered within two months, 51% explored career paths, 32% saved one. Still a strong result. Just not the one making the rounds this year. If a rollout plan is being built on a secondhand stat, this is the week to go check the primary source.

🍋 Lemonade Angle

What this week means for Lemonade's product & engineering L&D

1️⃣ Build the "why trust this" habit into code review — not a training module

Lemonade's engineers are already past the adoption question on AI coding tools. The real risk is the Wharton finding showing up quietly in PRs: an AI suggestion looks confident and syntactically clean, so it gets merged without a second look — especially risky on claims and underwriting logic. Concrete move: add a one-line PR template field for AI-assisted changes — "why do you trust this suggestion?" — cheap, fits in the existing flow of work, and it's the AI-fluency behavior Training Industry describes, not the AI-literacy behavior most engineers already have.

2️⃣ Treat the onboarding wiki as infrastructure, not content

A 3+ month engineering ramp is a static-content problem, and Bersin's maturity model puts static content on the bottom rung. Instead of another onboarding course, the higher-leverage move is a retrieval layer over what already exists — Slack history, incident runbooks, PR discussions, architecture decisions — that answers a new engineer's actual question in the moment. Same underlying pattern IBM and Eightfold are using for skills data: mine real signals instead of asking people to document things twice.

3️⃣ Mine GitHub activity for internal mobility signals — don't build a skills survey

Mastercard's real, verified numbers (62% registered, 51% explored paths) still show internal marketplaces get genuine engagement without needing inflated results. For ~200 people in product and engineering, a shared skills survey gets filled out once and goes stale. What won't go stale: signals already sitting in GitHub — languages touched, review patterns, on-call history — surfaced as "who's actually been working near X" when a squad needs to staff a project or someone's looking for a lateral move.
